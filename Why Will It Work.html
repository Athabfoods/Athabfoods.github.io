Supporting evidence:

Olds greenhouse - Alberta:

Check this guy out near olds Alberta.

Make sure to see just how warm his greenhouse is shortly after Christmas, in Alberta 2021. This was a very very cold month (and winter) up here. -30 average for most of both December and January.

Glazing and structure:

To benifit from natural ground warmth, the green house will be in the ground on the north side.

Living soil:

Did I say living soil? I sure did. In fact the soil is teeming with life. Some say that soil is so diverse in microbes that for every tablespoon there is billions of individual living somethings there.

And all those somethings must do something right?

We can say with certainty that having soil life greatly impacts plants positively just as much as we can say that not having life in the soil can be not so good for plants.

How good / not so good? That is complicated.

I have been discovering that it is not entirely the soil life that impacts how, and what, plants contribute to their environment.

What I do not understand is why adding certain amendments (soil ingredients) do the good things they do to plants and roots.

Each of those little guys I mentioned above do special things. When they digest soil contents they do so based on communications from the plants themselves. They also relay messages back to the plants through the ***mycelium network.

Plants:

You read that right. There are communications happening from the plants through the roots to the microbes and back.

Called ***exudates plants can signal anything from "I am sick and need such and such", to "I am deficient in this nutrient please send it", to "I have a pest infection send ______ to help me flush it out". Some plants even know when their own seed is present. When this happens they can kill off an area of their own roots in order to allow their seed best chances of survival.

Plants also ***have memory and can react (like ***venus fly trap) to events in their environment.

About microbes:

Some seem to make it so the roots can ***fix nitrogen better while others allow water to slowly absorb and distribute equally without running through quickly when the soil is parched.

A super useful thing is that most plants become cold resilient to the point of still being edible after half a dozen frosts (5-6).

Furthermore, they are able to take on more water than normal but somehow not become over watered no matter how much you water them.

Without rood damage or stunted growth plants are better able to protect themseves aaanndd they can also handle droughts just as well. Watering is less necessary when tha happens, saving costs.

They can somehow pick themselves up off the ground. In other words a plant that normally lay on the dirt, that you normally have to watch for food rot because of this no longer stays on the ground saving on wasted food.

They are also extremely wind resistant. We had a week of super windy days that were well over 100km/hr+++ for a couple days and our garden was good.

Other plants in our yard were not so lucky.

What ever it is that comes to life in the soil certainly helps plants in many ways.

I now know it does things that are almost to good to be true because year after year I have seen the same sorts of results wih my own eyes...
