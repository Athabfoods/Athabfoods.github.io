Plants needs and how that relates:

We all know that when a plant grows it needs some basic ingredients, right?
  • Things like ;energy - from the sun or lights
  • Food - through the root system
  • Water - from various sources
    • and
  • CO2 - From the air (what we exhale)

That's it right?

Actually no. Plants need the above things, these items below are certainly 'extras' in a way. That said, they do some amazing things when applied to plants, especially food, mmmmm umm!

What else can a plant need?:

  • Mirobes. Some call them 'myco's' or 'mycohyzzyl' which are fungi (mushroom).
  • We need a source of these microbes. Compost tea, humus, compost, store bought inocculant cocktail, micro brew, etc. are all excellent sources of soil life. When we put these things on, or in our soil we say we are 'inoculatibg it'.
  • Microbes need to eat something, so at first we need to give them food until they get going. As long as the root zone and soil immediately around the root zone are not disturbed the microbes will take hold. Until then we use sugar (de-sulfurated molasses) water with a humic acid blend and/or more inoculant / brew once a month.
  • They need good water and adequate sun light.

    Artificial lights work but are really expensive if paying for the equipment and the power bill.
  • A person should also monitor the propogation of microbes throughout the soil through a microscope. see microscope section - Coming soon.

Did you know that plants require Oxygen also?

They do. Not much, but they do.

In nature, it usually happens when rain falls. As raindrops fall they become concentrated with O2, when a drop hits the ground it takes that trapped oxygen into the ground with it. This oxygenates the roots which helps the roos do their routine better as well asding to the other positive effects.

Why? Oxygen promotes the roots to help plants process starches and sugars more efficiently. We all know how esential sugars are to photosynthesis. Well photosynthesis has another waste besides Oxygen, it's starch and thus very importat to process well.

A plabt sort of breathes once per day. During the day oxygen is exchanged out the leaves while absorbing carbon dioxide. This happens while the roots take in Oxygen and out put CO2. At night the plant stops most of its oxygen production and the roots turn day time produced starches back into sugars and other things the plant needs.

How does life get into the soil?:

Further work and observation shows the importance of compost in these relationships. This has brought us to the reality that we can revitalize a field just as easily with compost tea and a sprayer as we can with fertilizer. The difference is that fertilizers have to be reapplied often.

Giving soil back its life is only required in monoculture practices.

With these new strategies in the field we are able to take some soil life and grow it over time using crop rotation and planting certain plant types. More research needed here.

Compost tea is a brew made from a handful or two of good compost, placing that in a large barrel with water, some form of food for the microbes and letting that brew in the sun for a week or two. The result is a root innoculent that seeds soil life while vastly improving plant growth for a period of time afterwards.

Compost tea can be made cheeply, it costs significantly less than fertilizers and is sprayed just as easily using similar equipment.
