Plants needs and how that relates to my process:

We all know that when a plant grows it needs some basic ingredients, right?
  • Energy - From the Sun
  • Food - From the Roots
  • Water - From H2O
    • and
  • CO2 - From the air
Did you also know that plants require Oxygen in their root zone? This usually happens when rain falls. As it falls it becomes concentrated with O2, when it hits the ground raindrops bring this oxygenated water to the root where the roots take it in and use it. This matters to a plant quite a bit, yet no one mentions it.

We will ne oxygenating all waer supplies for feeding.

All these things are needed for a plant to do photosynthesis. Photosynthesis is a natural process that uses sunlight to drive a reaction where CO2 from the air, food from the roots, and water from the environment is turned into Oxygen for us and other animals to breathe as well as the energy for plants to grow (plant food).

Plants grow best under the sun, whether indoors or outdoors, it doesn't matter. For the best growth rate, the sun wins hands down because lights cost a lot and get to hot indoors to provide the same number of lumens (amount of energy per square foot) as the sun can.

Lighting is crazy expensive both in *LED set up costs (low power option) or in the amount of energy used (HID option). Plus bulbs only last so long and must be replaced eventually, even LEDs. Although LEDs are significantly longer life they still have a maximum service life.

This is why glazing (glass over greehouse) choice is crutial. For us it is FETF, described in projects below.

Indoor greenhouses cost a lot because the growing season is as long as the greenhouse can stay warm from the sun each year, meaning less months per year producing. Reduced output equals cost losses.

We will he growing year round, or darn close to it. I can say that at least 9-10 months of growth per year is achievable, at minimum.

Greenhouses could be heated but that costs a heap of money too.

We heat our greenhouse with thermal heat. See description below.

We may require lighting, if so, those costs are mitigated by on-site energy generation and storage, with energy credits, or by energy production close by (in some cases).

Composts vital role:

Further work and observation shows the importance of compost in these relationships. This has brought us to the reality that we can revitalize a field just as easily with compost tea and a sprayer as we can with fertilizer. The difference is that fertilizers have to be reapplied often.

Giving soil back its life is only required in monoculture practices.

With these new strategies in the field we are able to take some soil life and grow it over time using crop rotation and planting certain plant types. More research needed here.

Compost tea is a brew made from a handful or two of good compost, placing that in a large barrel with water, some form of food for the microbes and letting that brew in the sun for a week or two. The result is a root innoculent that seeds soil life while vastly improving plant growth for a period of time afterwards.

Compost tea can be made cheeply, it costs significantly less than fertilizers and is sprayed just as easily using similar equipment.

Living soil allows the roots to do what they are designed to do, support the plant. In nature this is automatic. Sadly for years we knew very little as to why this is the case. We now know that the roots communicate through mycelium

Microbes breakdown dirt transforming unusable nutrients and minerals into everything a plant needs to defend it self, feed it self, and keep itself healthy.
